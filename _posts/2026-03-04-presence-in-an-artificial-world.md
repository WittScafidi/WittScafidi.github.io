There's a moment in baseball that I think about a lot. You're in the box, the crowd is loud, the game is on the line, and a 90-mile-per-hour fastball is somewhere in that pitcher's hand. You have about 400 milliseconds from release to contact to decide what to do. There is no room in that window for anything except *right now*.

No notifications. No feed. No performance. Just the ball, your body, and the present moment.

I've been thinking about how rare that kind of presence has become — and how dangerous its absence is.

---

We live in an era of manufactured experience. Social media doesn't just document life; it shapes the decisions we make about how to live it. We choose restaurants based on how they'll photograph. We frame our opinions around how they'll land. We curate identities out of highlight reels and optimized bios, and then wonder why everything feels slightly hollow.

The digital world isn't inherently bad. I use data systems, automation tools, and analytics platforms professionally — technology is a genuine force multiplier. But there's a difference between *using* a digital layer and *living inside* one. The problem isn't the tools. It's when the simulation becomes the reference point for real life.

When you start performing your existence more than you're experiencing it, something gets lost. The meal you barely tasted because you were staging the photo. The conversation you half-heard because your attention was somewhere else. The feeling you didn't let yourself sit with because you were already thinking about how to express it.

We've outsourced our inner life to an audience. And audiences, by definition, need to be entertained.

---

I'm not immune to this. I've caught myself reaching for my phone in the first ten seconds of silence. Checking metrics on something I just made before I've even decided if I'm proud of it. Comparing my timeline to someone else's and feeling the particular low-grade anxiety that only exists because I know exactly what they had for breakfast.

But athletics gave me an antidote early. Sports, at the highest level, are brutally honest. You can't fake presence on a field. You either see the ball or you don't. You either execute or you don't. The outcome is immediate and real, and no amount of aesthetic framing changes the box score.

That feedback loop — real input, real consequence, no edit button — is something the digital world systematically eliminates. Online, everything is curated after the fact. You can delete the post, correct the take, reframe the narrative. The cost of being present and wrong feels higher, so we become more guarded, more performed, less *here*.

---

What does it actually mean to be present? I think it's simpler than we make it.

It means letting the moment be sufficient. Not reaching for documentation or validation before the experience has finished happening. It means tolerating boredom long enough to discover that boredom is actually the antechamber to every good idea you've ever had. It means being in a conversation with enough attention that the other person can feel it.

Presence isn't a spiritual concept — it's a discipline. And like most disciplines, it requires friction. It requires choosing the harder thing when the easier thing is one tap away.

The phone in your pocket is the most sophisticated attention-capture device ever engineered. Every product team behind every app in your dock has spent millions of hours figuring out how to interrupt you at exactly the right moment and pull you back in. That's not paranoia — that's their job description. Knowing that doesn't make you immune to it, but it does make the choice to disengage feel more intentional and less like deprivation.

---

I'm not arguing for a digital detox or a phone-free life. That's not realistic, and honestly it's not the point. The point is the ratio. The proportion of your day where you are the subject of your own experience versus an audience member watching a feed of everyone else's.

The most grounded people I've been around — coaches, mentors, teammates who have been through real things — share a quality I'd call *weight*. They're not performing. They're not optimizing. They're just genuinely *there*, with whatever's in front of them. It's rare enough now that it reads as a kind of charisma.

That's what we're losing. And I think we know it.

The world isn't going to slow down or get less artificial. The feeds will get more personalized, the simulations more convincing, the pull stronger. Which means presence — real, unmediated, full-attention presence — will become increasingly scarce.

Scarce things tend to become valuable.

Maybe the most important skill of the next decade isn't another technical competency or platform fluency. Maybe it's just the ability to put the phone down, look at what's in front of you, and *be there* — completely, without documentation, without audience.

The ball is in the air. What are you doing with the next 400 milliseconds?
